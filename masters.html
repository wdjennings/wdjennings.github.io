Simulation of Disease Epidemics

The spreading of diseases, rumours and opinions are often well-approximated by mathematical transmission models. My simulation is based on the SIR model of disease epidemics.

Tap on pictures to pause/play videos

Basic Model

My simulation starts with a single infected individual at the center of an otherwise healthy city. The city contains a huge number of individuals spaced out on a grid. Each individual can be in one of three states: susceptible, infected, or removed. This video shows an example simulation. Susceptible healthy individuals are green, infected individuals are red, and removed individuals are black.

Synergy

Some diseases behave either competitively or cooperatively. In the simple SIR model, the disease is neither cooperative nor competitive: an individual with two infected neighbours is twice as likely to be infected; an individual with three infected neighbours is three times as likely; and so on.

Cooperative Synergy

With cooperative synergy, infected individuals work together to infect new hosts. The infected hosts try to stay as close together as possible, encouraging the infection to spread outwards in clusters as can be seen here.

Competitive Synergy

With competitive synergy, infected invididuals work against one another. The infected hosts try to stay as far apart as possible, which discourages clusters. In this video, the infection progresses outwards in separate paths or tendrils.

Rewiring

My simulation allows the disease to randomly jump across the map, simulating infected individuals travelling from one physical location to another. For instance, a person travelling from home to their place of work, or an insect travelling across a field. This video shows how rewiring can dramatically increase the chance of an epidemic, although an interesting result was seen for cooperative diseases (see New Results below).

Novel Results

My results showed a clear dividing line between dangerous 'invasive' diseases and less-risky non-invasive ones. To determine whether a new disease is likely to be invasive, one can measure its infectivity and synergy in the safety of a lab, and observe which side of the dividing line it lies.

Somewhat counter-intuitively, the spread of cooperative diseases can actually be lessened by increasing the frequency of random rewiring jumps. This is because the random jumps prevent the disease from forming hot-spots, and stop the infected individuals benefiting from nearby cooperators.
