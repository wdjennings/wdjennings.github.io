Basic Model

My simulation starts with a single infected individual at the center of an otherwise healthy city. Each individual in the city can be in one of three states: susceptible, infected, or removed. This video shows an example simulation. Susceptible healthy individuals are green, infected individuals are red, and removed individuals are black.

Synergy

Some diseases behave either competitively or cooperatively. These two effects are collectively known as synergy.

Cooperative Synergy

With cooperative synergy, infected individuals work together to infect new hosts. The infected hosts try to stay as close together as possible, encouraging the infection to spread outwards in one extended wavefront, as can be seen here.

Competitive Synergy

With competitive synergy, infected invididuals work against one another. The infected hosts try to stay as far apart as possible, which discourages clusters. In this video, the infection progresses outwards in separate tendrils.

Rewiring

My simulation allows the disease to randomly jump across the map. For instance, an infected person travelling from home to their place of work, or an insect travelling across a field. Rewiring can dramatically increase the chance of an epidemic, as show in this video.

Other Network Shapes

My simulation can account for any shape of network. In the hexagonal network shown here, each individual can pass the infection to its six nearest neighbours. More advanced networks, for instance the non-regular spatial network of world airports, could easily be simulated by subclassing my Python class.

Experimental Results

My results showed a clear dividing line between dangerous 'invasive' diseases and less-risky non-invasive ones. To determine whether a new disease is likely to be invasive, one can measure its infectivity (\(\alpha\)) and synergy (\(\beta\)) in the safety of a lab, and observe which side of the dividing line it lies. Cooperative synergy has \(\beta \gt 0\), and competitive synergy has \(\beta \lt 0\), and larger values of \(\beta\) indicate a stronger effect.

Somewhat counter-intuitively, the spread of cooperative diseases can actually be lessened by increasing the frequency of random rewiring jumps. This is because the random jumps prevent the disease from forming hot-spots, and stop the infected individuals benefiting from nearby cooperators. This is only true of very cooperative diseases: I measured that the threshold of cooperativity for which rewiring stops the spread of disease is \(\beta \approx 3.0 \).
